Bridging clinical care and device design

Even during my dental training I was curious about how biomedical devices are developed and what goes on behind the scenes to make them safe and effective. That curiosity, and a desire to improve the tools I used every day, guided me through years of clinical practice where I organised outreach camps, treated more than 500 patients and learned how small design choices affect real people.

After organising over 20 health camps and managing hundreds of procedures, I realised that designing better devices was just as important as delivering care. Now, while studying for my M.Eng. at Cornell, I collaborate with engineers and physicians to translate clinical insight into dependable devices. This portfolio showcases that journey.

Projects

A snapshot of recent work spanning reliability, biodesign, simulation and custom implants. Click on each card to reveal more detail.

Insulin infusion pump reliability improvement

Explored how and why insulin pumps and infusion sets fail by combining process mapping, risk analysis and life data modelling, then translated those insights into reliability improvements.

Learn more
  • Documented the complete insulin delivery pathway to identify where faults originate and how they propagate through the system.
  • Used risk analysis to focus design improvements on the most critical components across electronics, mechanics and infusion hardware.
  • Applied life data and Weibull modelling to benchmark baseline performance and quantify the impact of proposed changes.
  • Streamlined investigation and corrective‑action workflows to cut defect rates and shorten troubleshooting cycles.

SIPOC‑style process map used to frame where reliability improvements would have the greatest patient impact.

Indicative reliability curves comparing baseline and improved system performance.

InfantGuard: GERD-sensing smart pacifier

Developed a pacifier‑based reflux monitor in collaboration with neonatologists and gastroenterologists at Weill Cornell. By sampling oesophageal reflux through tiny channels, it offers the first non‑invasive way to detect GERD at the bedside without disturbing feeding routines.

Learn more
  • Created a network of microfluidic channels and reservoirs that balances suction‑driven flow with manufacturability and infant comfort.
  • Built and refined prototypes using SLA‑printed, biocompatible resins to resolve issues such as channel collapse and support removal.
  • Planned bench tests that replicate infant suction patterns to validate sampling consistency and minimise cross‑contamination.

CAD rendering showing the pacifier shell and internal channel layout used during design reviews.

Paediatric catheter securement (Biodesign initiative)

Combined antimicrobial materials and improved securement to tackle CLABSI risk in neonatal care through careful human-factors analysis and risk management.

Learn more
  • Mapped workflows across multiple paediatric care settings to identify how accidental dislodgement and improper dressing lead to infection.
  • Explored material choices that offer antimicrobial protection while remaining gentle on fragile skin.
  • Defined verification requirements such as tensile strength, biocompatibility and application time to ensure both secure attachment and ease of use.
  • Balanced infection control and usability criteria to reduce risk priority numbers and move toward an integrated securement and antimicrobial solution.

Top Gun surgical simulation

Helped optimise laparoscopic training modules to better mirror operating‑room constraints, supporting surgeons in honing instrument handling and workflow awareness.

Learn more
  • Worked with surgical teams to translate procedures into realistic tasks, port placements and instrument paths for laparoscopic practice.
  • Refined simulator components to improve durability and provide more accurate tactile feedback.
  • Gathered feedback from trainees to improve ergonomics, adjust difficulty curves and enhance scoring mechanisms.

Patient‑specific implants for mucormycosis rehabilitation

Collaborated with the oral surgery department at Karnavati University to develop customised craniofacial implants from high‑resolution imaging, improving anatomical fit for patients recovering from mucormycosis.

Learn more
  • Generated detailed craniofacial models from CBCT data and incorporated surgeon‑defined constraints into the design.
  • Iterated designs with surgical teams to enhance fit accuracy and ensure implants could be placed safely and efficiently.
  • Established manufacturability and tolerance specifications consistent with ISO 13485 and documented regulatory requirements for custom Class I devices.

Clinical experience

Direct clinical exposure informs every design decision. Understanding how devices are used and misused helps translate engineering rigour into real-world reliability.

As part of my orthopaedic preceptorship at Guthrie Robert Packer Hospital, I observed a variety of orthopaedic, spinal and cranio‑maxillofacial surgeries. The exposure to different specialties revealed workflow bottlenecks, instrument handling patterns and sterile‑field constraints that feed back into my risk analyses and usability assessments.

During my dental training and practice at Karnavati School of Dentistry and in private clinics, I performed surgical extractions, restorative treatments and imaging studies under strict infection‑control protocols. I cared for more than 500 patients and organised over 20 health camps for underserved communities. These experiences taught me that even simple tools must withstand real‑world pressures—an insight I carry forward into device development.
